Story of the Braves

Overview

The Braves are the oldest continuously operating professional sports franchise in America, tracing their roots to the 1870s Boston Red Stockings. The team has fielded a squad every season since professional baseball began—something no other current MLB franchise can claim. From the Miracle Braves of 1914 to the 1995 World Series champs, from legends like Aaron and Spahn to moves from Boston to Milwaukee to Atlanta, their story is one of triumphs, droughts, and deep ties to baseball’s history.

Explore the timeline below to follow this rich and colorful journey.

Birth and Early Years

On January 20, 1871, Ivers Whitney Adams founded the Boston Red Stockings with $15,000 and help from Harry Wright, who had led the first pro team, the Cincinnati Red Stockings. Two months later, they became founding members of the National Association, the forerunner to the National League.

Like today’s Braves, Wright’s Red Stockings dominated early baseball—winning six of the first eight pennants, including 26 straight games in 1875 and an undefeated home record that same year.

The Boston Braves

By 1912, Boston’s National League team had become known as the Braves and was beginning to rebound after a rough decade. Following success in the 1870s and 1890s, the team entered a new era that brought two pennants (1914, 1948) and a World Series title in 1914—earned by the legendary "Miracle Braves." But by 1952, fan support had shifted heavily to the city’s American League team, the Red Sox.

The Milwaukee Braves

After 82 years in Boston, few expected the Braves to move to Milwaukee, home of their top minor league affiliate. Owner Lou Perini had promised to help Milwaukee get a Major League team, and most assumed it would be the struggling St. Louis Browns. But in spring 1953, citing declining fan support, Perini announced the Braves' move. National League owners unanimously approved, and 60,000 fans welcomed the team with a parade, beginning one of the happiest chapters in Braves history.

Moving to Atlanta

Declining fan support led the Braves to leave Milwaukee for Atlanta, just as it had in Boston. Milwaukee and Wisconsin pushed back with court battles and appeals, but the move became official in 1966. The team was welcomed with a parade and settled into Atlanta Stadium, where they soon found their winning form again.

The Team of the 90's

After a tough stretch in the 1980s, Atlanta fans were eager for a turnaround – and the Braves delivered in the 1990s. From the dramatic worst-to-first run in 1991 to the 1995 World Series title and strong seasons through 1999, the Braves became the most successful MLB team of the decade.

Atlanta Braves Legends

These six icons helped shape Braves history through record-breaking performances, unmatched leadership, and lifelong dedication to the team.

Hank Aaron

#44 – Outfielder
All-time home run leader (755), civil rights pioneer, and Braves icon.

Chipper Jones

#10 – Third Baseman
Hall of Famer, 1999 NL MVP, played entire career with the Braves.

Greg Maddux

#31 – Pitcher
4x Cy Young winner, master of control, and cornerstone of '90s Braves dominance.

Tom Glavine

#47 – Pitcher
2x Cy Young winner, World Series MVP in 1995, Hall of Famer.

John Smoltz

#29 – Pitcher
Dominated as both starter and closer. Cy Young, WS champ, HOF.

Bobby Cox

#6 – Manager
2,500+ wins, 1995 World Series champion, leader of 14 straight division titles.

Atlanta Braves Ballparks Through History

1871-1914:
South End Grounds

Capacity: 6,800
First Game: May 16, 1871
Last Game: August 11, 1914
Dimensions: 250 L, 445 LC, 440 RC, 255 R
Other Names: Walpole Street Grounds, The Grand Pavilion, Boston Baseball Grounds

  • Over 44 seasons, the team won 13 league titles and one World Series.
  • A 1894 fire destroyed South End Grounds, forcing the team to play at Congress Street Grounds during reconstruction.
  • The rebuilt South End Grounds was smaller, with two grandstand spires replacing the original six.
  • The Braves played at Fenway Park for a 1913 Memorial Day doubleheader and the final two games of the 1914 World Series.
  • In 1915, they continued playing some games at Fenway Park until Braves Field was finished.

1915-1952:
Braves Field

Capacity: 40,000
Groundbreaking: March 20, 1915
First Game: August 18, 1915
Last Game: September 21, 1952
Dimensions: 337 L, 355 LC, 390 C, 335 RC, 319 R
Other Names: The Wigwam, The Beehive, National League Park

  • Considered the largest ballpark in America when it opened.
  • The most distinctive feature was the vast expanse of outfield grass from foul line to foul line. It wasn't until 1928 that the fences were moved in and home runs became common.
  • The "Jury Box" bleachers in right field served as home to a rowdy group of fans who worshiped right fielder Tommy Holmes.
  • After the 1946 home opener, fans sat on wet green paint. The Braves apologized and covered over $6,000 in cleaning costs for about 5,000 fans.

1953-1965:
Milwaukee County Stadium

Approximate Cost: $5 million
Capacity: 36,011-43,768
Groundbreaking: March 20, 1915
First Game: August 18, 1915
Last Game: September 21, 1952
Dimensions: 337 L, 355 LC, 390 C, 335 RC, 319 R
Other Names: The Wigwam, The Beehive, National League Park

  • The first MLB park with lights and the first fully publicly funded.
  • By their 13th home game on May 20, the Braves had already surpassed their entire 1952 Boston attendance of 281,278.
  • With less than a month’s notice and no off-season marketing, the Braves drew a National League record 1,826,397 fans in 1953.
  • Hosted the World Series in 1957 and 1958.
  • The 1957 championship season drew 2,215,404 fans—a franchise record until 1992.

1966-1996:
Atlanta-Fulton County Stadium

Approximate Cost: $18 million
Capacity: 52,769
Groundbreaking: April 15, 1964
First Game: April 12, 1966
Last Game: October 24, 1996
Dimensions: 330 L, 375 LC, 402 C, 385 RC, 330 R
Other Names: The Launching Pad, Atlanta Stadium

  • Felipe Alou was the first Braves player to bat at Atlanta Stadium; his son Moises, with the Expos, was the last in regular season play.
  • The NFL’s Atlanta Falcons joined as co-tenants in 1966 and stayed until moving to the Georgia Dome in 1991.
  • A record 53,775 fans watched Hank Aaron hit his 715th home run on April 8, 1974. He also hit his 500th, 600th, and 700th home runs at the stadium.
  • The stadium was renamed Atlanta-Fulton County Stadium in 1976 when Ted Turner bought the team.
  • It hosted one World Series title (1995), four pennants, and seven division championships.
  • The stadium was imploded on August 2, 1997, and the site became a parking lot for Turner Field.

1997-2016:
Turner Field

Approximate Cost: $252 million
Capacity: 49,586
Groundbreaking: July 10, 1993 (for Olympic Stadium)
First Regular Season Game: April 4, 1997
Dimensions: 335 L, 380 LC, 400C, 390 RC, 330 R
Other Names: The Ted, Olympic Stadium

  • Turner Field was converted from the 80,000-seat Olympic Stadium used during the 1996 Olympic and Paralympic Games.
  • Construction of the Olympic Stadium took about three years; its conversion to Turner Field took just seven months.
  • When Turner Field was built, the majority of funding came from the Atlanta Committee for the Olympic Games (ACOG). No money came from the taxpayers.
  • Turner Field included 58 private suites, three party suites, and the 755 Club.
  • Beneath the turf was over three miles of drainage pipes with a one million-dollar state-of-the art computerized drainage system that could drain 102,000 gallons/hour of water from the field.
  • Today, Turner Field is Georgia State University’s football stadium.

2017-Present:
Truist Park

Approximate Cost: $672 million
Capacity: 41,084
Groundbreaking: September 16, 2014
First Regular Season Game: April 14, 2017
Dimensions: 335 L, 385 LC, 400C, 375 RC, 325 R

  • Truist Park was originally named SunTrust Park when it opened in 2017. It officially changed names on January 14, 2020, after the merging of SunTrust Bank and BB&T.
  • The Braves became the first team to build a ballpark and mixed-use development (The Battery Atlanta) at the same time, completing both in just 2.5 years.
  • The ballpark was developed by the Braves in partnership with the Cobb-Marietta Coliseum and Exhibit Hall Authority.
  • Due to its unique cantilever design, more seats are closer to the field than in any other MLB park, with no obstructed views.
  • The outfield wall consists of a variety of different heights beginning at 6 ft high at the left field foul pole to 16 ft high at the right.
  • The stadium features 4,000 premium seats and diverse group spaces like Below the Chop, The Home Depot Clubhouse, Hank Aaron Terrace, and the Xfinity Rooftop.
